AI Implementation

Why Companies Can't Make AI Value Stick

And how the organizations that win are redesigning work instead of automating it.

The Productivity Paradox Is Not New

When modern companies today struggle with getting value out of their AI investments, it is not a novel or a new problem. In 1987, the economist Robert Solow noticed something strange. America had spent billions on computers. Yet none of it was showing up in economic growth.

When factories first electrified in the 1880s, they swapped in electric motors and left everything else the same. For forty years, the productivity data barely moved at all. The gains only appeared when a new generation of owners redesigned the factory itself: open floor plans, assembly lines, new roles, new skills. The result was not a more efficient version of the old factory. It was a different kind of factory entirely.

AI is in the steam-engine-swap phase right now, except the cycle that took electricity forty years is compressing into years, or even months. In early 2026, a survey of thousands of CEOs found no measurable AI impact on productivity. The largest enterprise Copilot evaluation to date (20,000 employees, three months) reported significant user-reported time savings, but the report itself admitted it could not identify how that saved time was actually spent.

Lab studies and controlled pilots do show real gains, and sometimes dramatic ones. The question is not whether AI can make individuals faster. It is why those gains evaporate on the way to a business outcome.

Checking whether the tool is being actively used, often defined as one interaction per month, tells an organization nothing about whether work has actually changed or where freed capacity ended up. The value disappears in the spaces between roles, not within them.

As with electricity, capturing the value requires building new organizational capabilities.

That starts with redesigning work end-to-end: from what AI automates, through the behavioral change each role needs to make, to the new behaviors required for freed capacity to land somewhere valuable. If any link in that chain is undesigned, value leaks out.

Three Conditions

Three conditions that AI value has to pass through

Across AI programs, the same three behavioral conditions separate organizations that create real value from those that do not. The AI must improve the process. People must adopt new ways of working in a meaningful way. And the gains must land somewhere strategically valuable rather than dissipate.

But there is good news. AI generates interaction data that can reveal how work is happening on a user level. Not self-reported or surveyed. Turning this data into a diagnostic requires investment, but the behavioral signal has never been more insightful and granular.

CONDITION 01

Are you automating the right work?

The AI must improve the process

Before asking whether the AI output is good enough, there is a prior question: is this the right problem to be automating? A lot of AI deployments target tasks that are easy to automate rather than tasks whose automation would produce the most value. A team might get excellent AI-assisted meeting summaries, but if meeting summaries were never a bottleneck for that team's value delivery, the automation is irrelevant regardless of quality. The first job is to verify that the use case is connected to the capability the team exists to deliver.

Once that is established, the technology has to produce output that people in a specific role can actually build on. Not "does AI work in general" but "does it produce reliable output for this specific workflow?" That is a workflow-level question, and the answer can differ sharply across teams using the same tool.

CONDITION 02

Your highest-skilled people are your lowest adopters

People must adopt new ways of working

Here is where things come apart.

Picture a developer who has GitHub Copilot. They use it for autocomplete suggestions. The dashboard counts them as adopted. But they are still writing code the same way they did two years ago. They have not changed their process. They have added a feature to it.

Deeper adoption looks different. It means starting from the AI draft instead of a blank document. Trusting AI output enough to build on it rather than redo it. As AI moves toward agentic systems, it means something harder still: stepping out of the loop entirely, delegating to something you cannot supervise in real time.

Sometimes the barrier is workflow friction. The old way is easier, and the AI path requires extra steps. Without deliberate design making the new way the default, people stick with what they know.

The people best positioned to validate AI output, the senior practitioners, the domain experts, the people with fifteen years of experience, are consistently the lowest adopters. The resistance makes sense. A senior analyst watching AI generate a financial model in seconds is watching the market value of their core skill collapse in real time. Research on social threat (Rock, 2008) explains why AI hits harder than previous technologies: it can simultaneously threaten how people feel about their status, certainty, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness. Most technology changes trigger one of these. AI can trigger all five at once.

Most organizations treat the identity threat as a problem to manage. Research on organizational change suggests the opposite: treat it as intelligence. Help people see where their value is going, not where it is disappearing. That senior analyst's new value is knowing whether the model is right, what it means for the business, and what to do about it. Zuboff (1988) studied this exact transition in earlier waves of automation: organizations that invested in building the judgment capability produced more valuable workers. Those that just automated and expected people to figure it out produced worse ones.

You cannot make someone a good reviewer by telling them to review. Being excellent at building a financial model does not prepare someone for critically evaluating one. The judgment skill must be practiced, not explained. That means structured sessions where practitioners evaluate AI-generated work alongside their own, in settings where getting it wrong is safe. Pilot projects where people try the new role before it becomes a mandate (Ibarra, 1999). Not training programs that describe what the new role should look like.

CONDITION 03

The forty minutes nobody can account for

The gains must land somewhere strategically valuable

It's a Tuesday afternoon. A marketing analyst finishes a campaign report in twenty minutes instead of the usual hour. She looks at her calendar. Nothing is designed to happen next. So, she checks email, responds to a few messages, joins a meeting that could have been a Teams message. By five o'clock, the freed forty minutes have vanished.

This is not because people lack the discipline. It is a failure of design. When technology removes a task from someone's day, it removes the prompt for one behavior without supplying a prompt for the next.

This gap gets filled twofold:

First there is Jevons Paradox: when something becomes more efficient, people consume more of it. AI drafts emails in seconds, so people send more emails, which generates more emails for everyone else to respond to. AI makes it easy to run analyses, so teams produce more analyses without improving a single decision. AI generates presentations faster, so more decks get created, which means more meetings to present them and more people pulled in to review them. The efficiency gain becomes volume increase.

The second is Parkinson's Law: work expands to fill the time available. AI generates a first draft in minutes. Instead of shipping it, the person spends an hour refining because the time exists. The draft was good enough. The extra polish adds nothing the recipient will notice. AI produces an analysis in seconds. Instead of making the decision, the team requests three more scenarios because they're now cheap to produce. The task that was supposed to take an hour still takes an hour.

Both consume the freed capacity without producing actual business value. And both are preventable, but only through deliberate design. Without a pre-designed answer to "what do I do now?", people will either produce more of what they already know how to produce, or default into whatever is familiar. How hard it is to fill that vacuum depends on the distance between the old task and the desired new one. And on any given team, that distance is different for every role.

The Friction Map

Why the Same AI Deployment Creates Three Different Problems

A single AI deployment on a single team creates all three friction levels at once. The junior developer's transition from routine coding to AI-assisted coding might be low friction. The mid-level developer's transition from writing to reviewing AI output is medium friction, requiring a cognitive architecture they may never have practiced. The senior architect's transition from production to pure judgment and system design is high friction, with real identity-level resistance.

The more strategically valuable the destination, the higher the friction. The high-value transitions almost never happen through nudges alone. And when dozens of use cases across the organization all name "innovation" or "strategic work" as the destination, the destination itself becomes the bottleneck. Reallocation targets have finite absorptive capacity.

System-Level Thinking

Value disappears between roles

But even well-designed reallocation at the individual level misses the bigger picture. Value disappears between roles. When AI makes one role 30% faster and the downstream role cannot absorb the increased volume, the gain becomes a queue somewhere else.

When organizations look at the team as a system, something else becomes visible. Not just where value evaporates, but what new configurations become possible. Maybe junior developers absorb review tasks that senior architects used to handle, tasks they can manage now because AI assists their judgment. This frees the architects for system design work that was never possible before, because they were permanently buried in review queues. The value is not in any individual's freed hours. It is in the new configuration of the team, one that produces capabilities the organization did not have before.

A Diagnostic

Reading resistance

When people resist AI adoption, the question is not how to increase compliance. It is what the resistance is telling you. Getting the diagnosis wrong wastes the scarcest resource in any AI program: people's willingness to try again.

Condition What resistance looks like What it actually means
1. Process People try the tool, then stop. Feedback centers on output quality. Workarounds appear. The technology is not good enough for this workflow. Or people lack the AI literacy to get good output from it.
2. Adoption People use the tool but have not changed their process. Senior practitioners disengage. The role transition is too threatening or too undesigned. Professional identity is at stake.
3. Value People adopt and get faster, but business results do not move. Downstream queues grow. Freed capacity is evaporating. No designed destination. The value is disappearing between roles.
The Missing Piece

The behavioral design most programs are missing

The wrong starting point

Most AI business cases start from the tool: "Copilot saves developers five hours per week." That is a statement about a technology with no connection to a business outcome.

An honest business case starts from the capability, not the tool. Not "Copilot saves five hours per week" but "We need a 30% reduction in legacy system incidents over twelve months. What does AI need to deliver, what behaviors need to change, and where does freed capacity need to go to make that happen?"

That requires measuring differently than most organizations are used to.

The problem with self-reporting is not just imprecision. It can be backwards. METR's 2025 trial found developers who believed they were 20% faster with AI were actually 19% slower. AI interaction data can do better, but turning it into a role-level diagnostic requires joining it with org data and workflow systems. The infrastructure is what most organizations still have to build.

Design Framework

Architecting the paved path

Within any given team, a single AI deployment creates multiple friction levels at once. Each requires a different design, and all are happening simultaneously. One principle matters more than any other: the incentive structure has to change before the behavior does.

Friction Environment Capability Incentives
Low New behavior is the default path. AI literacy for this workflow. Don't penalize the AI path.
Medium Build the skill inside the work. Pair with practitioners. Evaluation judgment: practiced, not explained. New work shows up in reviews and development plans.
High Pilots, peer groups, protected time. Strategic thinking under ambiguity. Restructured metrics, career track, and compensation.

Webb and Sheeran's meta-analysis found that even when you successfully change someone's intention, the effect on their actual behavior is surprisingly small.

At low friction, the environment carries the intention. At medium and high friction, the environment, capability building, and incentive structure have to do almost all the work.

The Lesson from Electricity

Build the muscle now

The productivity paradox was never solved just by better technology. It was solved by the organizations that did something harder: they redesigned work itself, the roles, the flows, the skills, the meaning of expertise, and built capabilities the old system could never have supported.

The organizations building the reallocation muscle today are not just capturing more from their current AI investment. They are building the capability to capture more from every investment that follows.
